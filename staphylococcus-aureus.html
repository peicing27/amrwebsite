What is Staphylococcus aureus?

Staphylococcus aureus (Staph for short) commonly lives on the skin and inside the nose. It is the most dangerous of all of the many common staphylococcal bacteria. These gram-positive, sphere-shaped (coccal) bacteria often cause skin infections but can cause pneumonia, heart valve infections, and bone infections.

Why should you care?

Some strains of Staph are resistant to multiple antibiotics, most famously for methicillin. Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) is an antimicrobial-resistant type of S. aureus that is resistant to currently available beta-lactam antibiotics including penicillins (e.g., penicillin, amoxicillin), “anti-staphylococcal” penicillins (e.g., methicillin, oxacillin), and cephalosporins (e.g., cephalexin). When a patient has a skin infection, it may very likely be MRSA. MRSA was a hospital acquired infection. Recent data suggest that MRSA in the community is increasing

What can be done?

Patient education is a critical component of MRSA case management. Healthcare professionals should educate patients, caretakers and, when possible, household members on methods to avoid MRSA transmission to close contacts.
