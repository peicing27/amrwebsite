What is Pseudomonas aeruginosa?

P. aeruginosa is a gram-negative, motile, rod shaped bacterium. This opportunistic pathogen is commonly associated with hospital-acquired infection, most notably in immunocompromised individuals. Pseudomonas aeruginosa accounts for 10% of all hospital acquired infections. Specifically, this bacterium is one of the most frequently recovered pathogen from intensive care unit (ICU) patients.

Why should you care?

A variety of human infections are commonly associated with this bacterium:

  1. Urinary tract infections
  2. Ocular infections
  3. Ventilator associated pneumonia
  4. Ear infections
  5. Surgical site infection
  6. Respiratory infections

Why is it hard to treat P. aeruginosa infections?

P. aeruginosa is resistant to commonly used antimicrobial agents. Treatment against P. aeruginosa infections includes the antimicrobials below.

  • Ceftazidime
  • Cefepime
  • Carbapenems
  • Aminoglycosides
  • Fluoroquinolones
  • Extended-spectrum penicillins
